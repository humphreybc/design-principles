30 Principles of Design

site by Benjamin Humphrey

  • 80/20 Rule

    A high percentage of effects in any large system are caused by a low percentage of variables. Otherwise known as the Pareto Principle or law of the vital few.

  • Advance Organizer

    An instructional technique that helps people understand new information in terms of what they already know. There are two kinds, expository, and comparative.

  • Affordance

    A quality of an object, or an environment, which allows an individual to perform an action. For example, a knob affords twisting, and perhaps pushing, while a cord affords pulling. Push and pull doors are often cited as good examples.

  • Alignment

    The placement of elements such that edges line up along common rows or columns, or their bodies along a common center.

  • Anthropomorphic Form

    A tendency to find forms that appear humanoid or exhibit human-like characteristics appealing.

  • Area Alignment

    Alignment based on the area or visual ‘weight’ of elements versus the edges of elements.

  • Chunking

    A technique of combining many units of information into a limited number of units or ‘chunks’ so that the info is easier to process and remember. A common example is the use of bullet points in presentations.

  • Closure

    A tendency to see a set of individual elements as a single recognizable pattern, rather than multiple individual elements.

  • Cognitive Dissonance

    A tendency to seek consistency among attitudes, thoughts, and beliefs, or ‘the feeling of discomfort when simultaneously holding two or more conflicting cognitions.’

  • Common Fate

    Elements that move in the same direction are perceived to be more related than elements that move in different directions or are stationary.

  • Comparison

    A method of illustrating relationships and patterns in system behaviors by representing two or more system variables in a controlled way.

  • Figure-Ground Relationship

    Elements are perceived as either figures (objects of focus) or ground (the rest of the perceptual field). Characteristics of figures include defined shape, seeming closer with a clear location, below the horizon line, and lower regional placement.

  • Flexibility-Usability Tradeoff

    As the flexibility of a system increases, the usability of the system decreases. Accommodating flexibility requires satisfying a larger set of requirements, which results in complexity and usability compromises.

  • Forgiveness

    The ‘undo’ option. Designs should help people avoid errors altogether, and minimize the negative consequences of errors when they inevitably do occur. Good affordances, reversibility of actions, confirmation of intent, and warnings are all concepts that contribute to forgiveness.

  • Gutenberg Diagram

    A diagram that describes the general pattern followed by the eyes when looking at evenly distributed, homogeneous information.

  • Iconic Representation

    The use of pictorial images (icons, symbols) to improve the recognition and recall of signs and controls.

  • In-attentional Blindness

    The failure to cognitively process a stimulus that is presented in clear view, leaving the observer without any awareness or memory of the stimulus.

  • The Isolation Effect

    A phenomenon of memory in which noticeably different or unique things are more likely to be recalled than common things.

  • Law of Pragnanz

    A tendency to interpret ambiguous images as simple and complete, versus complex and incomplete.

  • Legibility

    The visual clarity of text, generally based on the size, typeface, contrast, text block, and spacing of the characters used.

  • Natural Mapping

    A relationship between controls and their movements or effects. Good mapping between controls and their effects results in greater ease of use.

  • Operant Conditioning

    A technique used to modify behavior by reinforcing desired behaviors, and ignoring or punishing undesired behaviors.

  • Picture Superiority Effect

    This one’s simple. Pictures are remembered better than words.

  • Proximity

    Elements that are close together are perceived to be more related than the elements further apart.

  • Recognition Over Recall

    Memory for recognizing things is better than memory for recalling things.

  • Serial Position Effect

    A phenomenon of memory in which items presented at the beginning and end of a list are more likely to be recalled than the items in the middle of a list.

  • Signal-to-noise Ratio

    Ratio of relevant to irrelevant information in a display.

  • Storytelling

    A method of creating imagery, emotions, and understanding of events through an interaction between a storyteller and an audience.

  • Visibility

    The usability of a system is improved when its status and methods of use are clearly visible.

  • Weakest Link

    The use of a weak element that will fail in order to protect other elements from damage.
